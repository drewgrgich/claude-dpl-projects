## CONCEPT

**The Burrow** is a drafting game where every player simultaneously steals from their neighbors.

Nine rounds. Three passes per round. A 3-column burrow that you fill card by card. The same suits together score multipliers. The same consecutive ranks score more. Split-suit cards are wild — but stealing the right card from your neighbor's hand is the real game.

**The core tension:** Take the card you need, or grab the card your neighbor was about to get. What you pass is as important as what you keep.

---

## COMPONENTS

- **66-card Hamsters & Monsters deck** (ranks 0–10 in six suits; ~20 split-suit cards where one rank appears in two suits)
- No additional components required

---

## SETUP

1. Shuffle the deck.
2. **Deal 3 cards face-down to each player.** These are your hand.
3. Place **1 card face-up** in the center of the table. This is the **Center Starter** — a shared card that no one owns. (In 5-player games, skip the Center Starter.)
4. Remaining cards = **Draw Pile**, face-down beside the center.
5. **Oldest player begins.**

**Your Burrow:** An empty grid of **3 columns** (labeled A, B, C), each with **3 slots** — 9 slots total.

```
Column A  |  Column B  |  Column C
----------|------------|----------
  [    ]  |  [    ]    |  [    ]
  [    ]  |  [    ]    |  [    ]
  [    ]  |  [    ]    |  [    ]
```

## HOW TO PLAY — THE WHEEL DRAFT

The game lasts **9 rounds**. Each round has **3 passes**.

A **pass** means: look at your hand, choose 1 card to keep, and pass your **remaining hand** to the player on your LEFT simultaneously. You receive a new hand from the player on your RIGHT at the same time.

### One Round, Step by Step

**Pass 1 of 3:**
- Everyone: choose 1 card from your 3-card hand to keep. Place it face-down in front of you.
- Pass your remaining 2 cards to the player on your LEFT at the same time.
- Receive 2 cards from the player on your RIGHT.

**Pass 2 of 3:**
- Everyone: choose 1 of the 2 cards you just received. Add it to your face-down pile.
- Pass your remaining 1 card to the player on your LEFT.
- Receive 1 card from the player on your RIGHT.

**Pass 3 of 3:**
- Everyone: you now hold exactly 1 card. Add it to your face-down pile. (No passing this time.)

**Placement:** After all 3 passes, everyone simultaneously **reveals** their 3 drafted cards and places each one into any **empty slot** in their Burrow grid (Column A, B, or C). You choose which card goes where.

**Round end:** After all players have placed, discard all revealed cards (face-up where everyone can see). Draw **1 card** from the Draw Pile and place it in the center slot (if empty). Then each player draws **3 fresh cards** from the Draw Pile for the next round.

### The Draw Pile Runs Out

When the Draw Pile has fewer than (players × 3) cards remaining, players draw as many as they can. When a player's hand is empty before round 9, they score their Burrow as-is (remaining slots count as 0). The game ends when all players have exhausted their hands or when all Burrows are complete.

## SCORING

After 9 rounds, every Burrow is complete. Score all 3 columns.

**For each column, calculate:**
1. **Base score** = sum of all ranks in the column (0–10 = face value; J/Q/K/A = 10)
2. **Apply doubling** (see below)
3. **Add center bonus** (Column B only): +5 points

### When Does a Column Double? (×2)

A column's score is **doubled** when all 3 cards in that column share **the same dominant suit**.

- **3 single-suit cards of the same suit** → Doubled ✓
- **2 single-suit cards of the same suit + 1 split-suit card that includes that suit** → Doubled ✓
- **Mixed suits, including split-suit cards** → Not doubled ✗
- **Split-suit cards without a matching single-suit majority** → Not doubled ✗

### Quick Scoring Reference

| Column Contents | Score |
|----------------|-------|
| Mixed suits (no column qualifies for doubling) | Sum of ranks |
| All 3 cards are the same suit (×3 single, or ×2 + split) | Sum × 2 |
| Column B center bonus | +5 (after doubling) |

**No other multipliers. No stacking. One doubling at most.**

---

## SPLIT-SUIT CARDS

Split-suit cards (e.g., **5♥5♣**) are powerful flexible tools. They count as **both suits simultaneously** for all purposes:

- **For suit-doubling:** A column with 2 Hearts and 1 Hearts/Clubs counts as **3 Hearts** → score × 2
- **For placement:** You may place a split-suit card in any column regardless of what other suits are there

**Strategic note:** Split cards don't score higher on their own — they give you *options*. A split card in Column A lets you complete that column's suit set, but it can't then also complete Column B's suit set. Choosing where to place split cards is one of the game's key decisions.

---

## END GAME

After 9 rounds, all Burrows are complete.

Each player scores all 3 columns. **Highest total wins.**

**Tiebreaker:** Most columns doubled (×2). Still tied: highest individual column score. Still tied: tied players share the win.

## PLAYER SCALE

### 2 Players
- Each player manages **2 Burrows**: their own and a "guest" Burrow built simultaneously from the same draft.
- Both Burrows score independently; each player's combined total (Real Burrow + Guest Burrow) is their score.
- **The twist:** After round 5, each player secretly chooses which of their 2 Burrows is their "real" one. At scoring, announce simultaneously.
- Drafting remains identical (2 passes per round means cycling hands more slowly — add 1 extra pass per round so the wheel completes properly).

**2P variant (simpler):** Each player builds 1 Burrow only. Use a 2-card hand (not 3) and 9 passes per round to fill 9 slots in exactly 9 rounds. Faster, less complex.

### 3 Players
- Standard rules as written.
- 3-player draft is the sweet spot — quick cycling, strong draft hate, no runaway leaders.

### 4 Players
- Standard rules.
- Draw Pile runs out around round 7. After that, players draft with remaining hand cards only.
- 4-player games are tight and aggressive; suit-doubling is harder to achieve when opponents are stealing your suits.

### 5 Players
- **No Center Starter card.**
- Each player starts with **3 cards**; draft 3 cards per round as normal.
- Fewer total passes needed before the deck runs out — more reactive, less planning.
- Fastest player count; best for people who want a shorter game.

## VARIANTS

### Draft-Attack (adds confrontation)
When you complete a column with all 3 cards placed in the same round (all 3 cards placed in Column A, B, or C simultaneously), you may **demand 1 card** from any one opponent's next draft selection. That opponent shows you the card they draft before placing it; you take it and place it in your Burrow instead. Your opponent re-drafts one card from their hand to fill that slot.

*This variant rewards aggressive placement timing and creates memorable "theft" moments.*

### Chaos Burrow (simpler scoring)
Remove all suit-doubling. Score each column as its raw sum only. Column B gets +5. No suit powers, no doubling. Fastest scoring. Good for families or first-time players.

### Speed Burrow (faster game)
Reduce grid to **2 columns × 3 slots** (6 cards per Burrow). Game ends in 6 rounds. 12–15 minutes. All other rules identical.

## DESIGNER NOTES

**Why wheel drafting?**
Simultaneous pick-and-pass drafting is one of the most satisfying mechanics in card games. You get to choose — but what you don't take, you give away. The person on your left gets what you passed. The person on your right gets what you chose not to take. Every pass is a small negotiation.

**Why 3 passes per round?**
Three passes per round means you draft 3 cards per round from a 3-card hand. Nine rounds = 9 cards = 9 slots. The math works cleanly. The 3-pass structure also means the wheel doesn't complete a full rotation in a single round — cards you passed in Pass 1 come back to you in Pass 4 of the *next* round, creating delayed feedback loops.

**Why suit-doubling and not rank runs?**
Rank runs were in earlier drafts and created interesting decisions, but the scoring math (base × suit × run = several different multipliers) was too heavy for a game that wants to be scored "at a glance." Suit-doubling (×2 or not ×2) is a binary, readable decision. Split cards add nuance without adding math.

**What about card powers?**
Suit powers were in earlier drafts (Hearts: draw extra cards; Diamonds: peek a grid; Clubs: swap placed cards; Spades: redistribute top cards). They added texture but also added rules overhead and slowed play. In the clean version, *the cards themselves* are the only powers. What you draft, where you place it, and what you pass is all the game needs.

**The center bonus (+5 to Column B)**
A small but meaningful tiebreaker. It makes the middle column attractive — and since you place cards one at a time, sometimes you take the card that lands in Column B even when Column A or C would score better. A small "worth it sometimes" decision beats a "always/never" rule.

## INPIRATIONS

- **Ticket to Ride** (route-building as personal creation) — the "my thing is getting better" loop
- **7 Wonders** (simultaneous card selection and tableau building) — the pick-and-pass structure
- **Sushi Go!** (simultaneous drafting, pattern scoring) — the quick-round scoring feel
- **Century: Golem Edition** (engine-building with simple conditions) — the satisfaction of completing a set

---

## INTENDED AUDIENCE

**Primary:** Strategy gamers, deck-builders, Euros fans. The drafting mechanic rewards reading opponents and planning 2–3 turns ahead.

**Secondary:** Couples and small groups who want a 20-minute game with real decisions but no heavy rules overhead.

**Avoid if:** You want a game with no player interaction (The Burrow is hostile by design — what you take, they lose). Also avoid if you find scoring multiplication mentally taxing.

**Age:** 10+. Math is basic addition and ×2 only.

## MANUFACTURING NOTES

- **66-card deck only.** No additional components.
- **Reference card:** A single-sided scoring reference card (3 column diagrams + doubling rules) would dramatically help first-time players. Ideally sized at 2.5" × 3.5" (card-sized) so it can sit on the table without being confused for a game card.
- **Playmat (optional):** A 3-column grid on a playmat would help younger players with placement but is not required for adults.
- **Art direction:** Each suit could have a distinct room theme — Hearts is a cozy nest, Spades is a storage chamber, Diamonds is a food vault, Clubs is a play tunnel. The split cards show two room types blended together.
- **Box:** Standard small-cardgame box. 66 cards + rules + optional reference card fit in a 4" × 5.5" box easily.

## COMPARISON TO EXISTING H&M GAMES

| Feature | Bid Brawl | Rampage Run | **The Burrow** |
|---------|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Core mechanic** | Blind simultaneous bidding | Press-your-luck streak building | **Wheel drafting + tableau** |
| **Cards in play** | Cards flow to other players | Cards stay in center streak | **Cards go into personal grid** |
| **Hidden information** | Hidden bids | None (open cards) | **Hidden card selection (during draft)** |
| **Player interaction** | Economic (feeding) | Competitive (race to bank) | **Direct theft (taking from neighbor's options)** |
| **Engine building** | No | No | **Yes — columns compound over 9 rounds** |
| **Endgame scoring** | Market + feeding | Banked points | **Column scoring + doubling** |
| **Setup time** | Medium | Short | **Very short — deal 3, go** |
| **Teach difficulty** | Medium | Easy | **Easy (wheel draft is intuitive)** |
| **Downtime** | Low (simultaneous) | Medium | **Low-medium (3 passes, then simultaneous place)** |
| **Luck vs. skill** | 60/40 | 50/50 | **55/45 (draft hate mitigates bad draws)** |
| **Replayable tension** | High | High | **Very high (suit distributions vary every game)** |

**Key differentiator:** Bid Brawl is about *what you bid*. Rampage Run is about *when you stop*. The Burrow is about *what you take and what you give*. All three are mechanically distinct. The Burrow is the only one where players build a personal visible structure over time.
