# 🏔️ SUMMIT SCRAMBLE

### A Climbing Race for 3–5 Players

**System:** Hamsters & Monsters Core Deck (66 cards) · **Time:** 20 Minutes · **Ages:** 10+

------

## What's Going On Here?

Once a year, beneath stadium lights and the distant crackle of confetti cannons, the six factions gather for the **Summit Scramble** — a vertical sprint up the tallest peak in the fuzzy multiverse. No ropes. No safety nets. Just cards, cunning, and the overwhelming desire to reach the top before everyone else.

You're a climber. Your hand of cards is your energy. Every card you play propels you higher — but you've only got so much fuel. Play too aggressively and you'll burn out before the final push. Play too cautiously and you'll watch someone else plant their flag while you're still looking for a foothold.

The first player to play their last card **reaches The Summit**. Everyone else keeps climbing until they finish or collapse. The slowest climber accumulates **Fatigue** (measured in Zzz's, obviously). Play multiple rounds. The player with the *lowest* Fatigue after someone hits the limit wins the Championship.

*Shake hands after. It's a rule.*

------

## What You Need

Just the **Hamsters & Monsters deck** (66 cards — six factions, numbered 0 through 10 each).

**The Six Factions:**

| Color    | Faction            | Symbol           |
| -------- | ------------------ | ---------------- |
| 🔴 Red    | Super-Dupes        | Shield           |
| 🟠 Orange | Finders-Keepers    | Magnifying Glass |
| 🟡 Yellow | Tinkerers          | Spanner          |
| 🟢 Green  | Prognosticationers | Crystal Ball     |
| 🔵 Blue   | Magicians          | Top Hat          |
| 🟣 Purple | Time Travelers     | Backward Clock   |

Something to track **Fatigue** — a score pad, a phone, scratches on the table. Hamsters aren't picky.

## Setup

1. **Shuffle** all 66 cards.
2. **Deal** starting hands based on player count:

| Players | Cards Each |
| ------- | ---------- |
| 3       | 15         |
| 4       | 13         |
| 5       | 11         |

1. Place any remaining cards face-down as **the Trail** — the path ahead. Some abilities will draw from here.
2. Leave space for **Base Camp** — a face-up discard pile. It starts empty. When tricks are won, played cards tumble back down here.
3. Pick someone to go first. (Winner of the last game goes first. First game? Shortest player. We don't make the rules. Wait — yes we do.)

------

## The One Rule That Runs Everything

On your turn, you either **play cards that beat what's on top** — or you **pass**.

That's the whole game. Everything else is just details about *what* you can play and *when*.

Here's the rhythm: One player **leads** by playing a card formation. Going clockwise, everyone else must either **beat it**with the same type of formation but higher — or **pass** and sit this one out. Once everyone else has passed, the last player standing **wins the trick**, discards everything to Base Camp, and leads the next one.

First to empty your hand reaches The Summit. Last place gets sleepy.

------

## Formations (How to Climb)

Every play is a **formation** — a specific arrangement of cards. To beat a formation, you must play the **same type** with a **higher rank**. You can never change the formation type mid-trick. If someone led with a single card, everyone must play singles (or pass). If someone led with a pair, everyone must play pairs (or pass).

Here are the four formations, from simplest to wildest:

------

### 🧗 Solo Sprint (Single Card)

The simplest play. One card. Beat it with a higher-ranked card.

**Tie-breaker:** If two cards share the same rank, the **faction hierarchy** decides: Red > Orange > Yellow > Green > Blue > Purple. (Think of it as ROYGBP — the rainbow picks the winner.)

*When two climbers hit the same height, the higher-tier faction claims the ledge.*

> **Example:** Someone plays a Green 7. You can beat it with *any* 8, 9, or 10 — or a Red, Orange, or Yellow 7 (because those factions outrank Green).

------

### ⚡ The Surge (Set of 2 or 3)

Two or three cards of the **same rank**, played together.

Beat it with the same quantity at a **higher rank**. A pair of 6s beats a pair of 4s. A triple of 9s beats a triple of 7s. Faction doesn't matter for Surges — only rank.

*Team formations move faster, but require coordination. And trust. Mostly trust.*

------

### 🌼 The Daisy Chain (Run of 3+)

Three or more cards of **consecutive ranks** — like 3-4-5 or 7-8-9-10. Mixed factions are fine. (Inter-faction cooperation is encouraged during vertical emergencies.)

Beat it with a run of the **same length** and a **higher top card**. A 4-5-6 beats a 3-4-5. A 6-7-8-9 beats a 5-6-7-8.

*Link paws, don't look down, and absolutely do not let go of the hamster in front of you.*

------

### 🎆 The Confetti Cannon (Bomb — 4 of a Kind)

Four cards of the **same rank**. The ultimate celebration.

The Confetti Cannon is special:

- It can be played **on any formation, at any time** — even if it's not your turn and even if you've already passed this trick
- It **immediately ends the trick**. No responses. No comebacks
- The Cannoneer **wins the trick and leads the next one**

If two players fire Cannons on the same trick (rare but glorious), the **higher rank** wins. Same rank? Faction hierarchy breaks the tie. (This has never happened in recorded hamster history. But we planned for it.)

**Power Check:** Yes, Cannons trigger Faction Abilities — same as any winning formation. If the Cannon's rank is 6 or higher, choose **one** faction from among the four cards to activate. One formation, one trigger. You don't get four Rotations. (You're welcome, everyone at the table.)

*Somewhere in the bleachers, confetti rains on a hamster who was just trying to eat popcorn.*

------

### 🍌 The Trip-Up (Rank 0 vs. Solo 10)

Here's where it gets sneaky. A single **Rank 0** card can beat a **Solo Rank 10** — and just like the Confetti Cannon, it can come from **anyone, at any time**, even if they've already passed.

Someone plays a solo 10 to seize control. The table freezes. Does anyone have a 0? Two seconds of silence. Nervous eye contact. Then either the 10 stands — or someone slaps down a 0 and the room explodes.

**How it works:**

- Whenever a **Solo Rank 10** is the current top card, any player may play a single Rank 0 as an interrupt
- The trick **ends immediately** — the Trip-Up player wins and leads next
- Like the Cannon, this works even if you've already passed this trick

**Restrictions:**

- Only works on **Solo Sprints** (a single 10). Cannot interrupt a Surge, Daisy Chain, or Cannon
- **Cannot** interrupt someone reaching The Summit — if a player goes out on a solo 10, the trick ends before anyone can respond
- The tripped card **never wins the trick**, so its Faction Ability does not trigger. You don't get to use your power from the floor

> **Why can't double or triple 0 beat double or triple 10?** A trip-up stops one climber — one paw stuck out at the worst moment. A coordinated team (Surge) or a linked chain braces each other. You can't trip a conga line. Mechanically, this keeps the 0 as a surgical tool against lone finishers rather than a "hoard all the 0s" degenerate strategy.

> **The mind game:** Every solo 10 is now a gamble. Play it early when more 0s are in circulation? Or hold it for late when 0s have likely been spent? And for the player *holding* the 0 — do you trip now and seize the lead, or save your insurance policy for later? Using the 0 offensively means losing it defensively. That's a real decision.

------

## The Race (Turn by Turn)

Now you know what to play. Here's when and how.

------

### 1. Take Point (Lead)

The trick winner — or the starting player at the beginning of a round — **leads** by playing any legal formation from their hand.

This is your moment. You set the pace. You choose the terrain. Singles? Pairs? A long Daisy Chain that only you can beat? Your call.

------

### 2. Keep Up (Follow)

Going clockwise, each player must do one of two things:

- **Advance:** Play the same formation type at a higher rank. Beat what's on top.
- **Conserve Energy:** Pass. You're out for the rest of this trick.

**The Burnout Rule:** Once you pass, you're done until the next trick starts. No take-backs, no "wait actually I had something." You committed to resting. Rest.

*(Exception: Confetti Cannons and Trip-Ups. Those are interrupts — they can fire from anywhere, anytime, even after passing. Because confetti and banana peels wait for no one.)*

------

### 3. Clearing the Ledge (Winning a Trick)

When all other players have passed, the last player standing **wins the trick**.

Here's what happens:

1. **All played cards** go face-up to Base Camp
2. **Power Check:** If the winning card is **Rank 6 or higher**, the winner triggers their **Faction Ability** (see next section)
3. **Lead again:** The winner plays the next formation

> **Key detail:** The winning *card* determines the power, not the winning *player*. If you win with a Red 8, you trigger the Super-Dupes ability — regardless of what other cards you played alongside it. For Surges and Cannons (pairs/triples/quads), the rank of the set determines the power threshold, and you choose which card's faction to activate. One formation, one trigger.

> **Trail Exhaustion:** The instant you need to draw or look at cards from the Trail and it's empty (or has fewer cards than you need), **stop and reshuffle Base Camp into a new Trail immediately** — then finish your draw or ability. Don't wait for the trick to end. Don't wait for your turn to finish. The mountain rebuilds itself mid-climb. *(This happens frequently at 5 players. It's normal. Keep moving.)*

------

### 🏁 Reaching The Summit (Going Out)

When you play your **last card**, you immediately reach The Summit.

- The trick **ends instantly** — no Cannons or Trip-Ups may be fired in response. Going out is sacred
- You're done. You've summited. Flex appropriately
- The player to your left leads the next trick
- **Play continues** until everyone has finished (or given up and fallen asleep)

------

## Faction Abilities

This is where Summit Scramble gets sharp. Powers only trigger when you **win a trick** with a card ranked **6, 7, 8, 9, or 10**. Win with a 5 or lower? No power. Just the trick.

*(If you've played Get Stuffed, this threshold is familiar. Same deck, same cutoff, different powers.)*

------

### 🛡️ Super-Dupes (Red) — THE ROTATION

*"Strength and Let Me Help"*

The triggering player announces a direction — **left or right**. All players simultaneously choose **1 card** from their hand and pass it to the player in that direction.

Everyone gives, everyone receives. Nobody is safe. That carefully hoarded pair of 9s? One of them just got shipped to your rival. The Daisy Chain you were saving? Someone just sent you a 0 that doesn't fit.

The direction is the decision. Pass left to dump a brick on the player who's about to win. Pass right to punish the one who just Trip-Upped you. The Rotation is chaos — but it's chaos you *aimed*.

> **3-Player Rule (Mandatory):** At 3 players, The Rotation must alternate directions — if the last Rotation went left, the next must go right. Choosing a direction at 3 players is choosing a *specific person* to dump on, and that's targeted aggression, not disruption. The alternation keeps it honest. At 4–5 players, direction choice is unrestricted.

------

### 🔍 Finders-Keepers (Orange) — SCOUT

*"Just Dibs Mine"*

Look at the **top 2 cards** of the Trail. You may **swap** 1 card from your hand with 1 of them. Return any unchosen card(s) to the top of the Trail.

You looked. You found something better. Dibs. Your hand size doesn't change, but its *quality* just improved — and you got a peek at what's coming. The Finders-Keepers don't take more than they need. They just make sure what they *have*is exactly what they want.

------

### 🔧 Tinkerers (Yellow) — STREAMLINE

*"Too slow!"*

Discard **1 card** from your hand to Base Camp.

Wait — you *want* to discard? Yes. This is a racing game. Every card in your hand is weight. Streamline drops dead weight and gets you closer to The Summit. A free discard after winning a trick is *acceleration*.

*Brakes are optional. Ethics are flexible. Speed is mandatory.*

------

### 🔮 Prognosticationers (Green) — RECALIBRATE

*"I Knew That!"*

Draw **1 card** from the Trail, then discard **2 cards** from your hand to Base Camp.

You saw the future. You trimmed accordingly. Recalibrate is *precision acceleration* — you pick up one new card, see what the universe is offering, and then shed two pieces of dead weight. Net result: your hand shrinks by one *and* gets better. Where Streamline is raw speed (dump and go), Recalibrate is calculated speed (peek, assess, then dump smarter).

The crystal ball works. The Prognosticationers just won't tell you *how*.

------

### 🎩 Magicians (Blue) — THE REVELATION

*"Sleight of Paw"*

Choose a player who has **fewer cards than you**. They reveal their hand **to you only** (nobody else peeks). Take **1 card**from them. Then give them **1 card** from your hand.

You just gathered intel *and* swapped resources. The card you give them might be useless to you and devastating to them — or maybe you're planting a brick in their hand while stealing their finisher.

**Important:** You can only target someone with fewer cards. You're scouting the leaders, not bullying the stragglers.

------

### ⏰ Time Travelers (Purple) — RECLAIM

*"I've already done this 1000x"*

Swap **1 card** from your hand with **any card** in Base Camp.

That perfect card someone played three tricks ago? It's yours now. The 0 you discarded earlier because you didn't need it? Turns out you do. Time Travelers don't make mistakes — they just haven't finished correcting them yet.

------

> **Timing your powers is the game within the game.** The Rotation disrupts everyone in a direction you chose. Streamline and Recalibrate are both acceleration — one fast, one precise. Scout upgrades your hand from the unknown; Reclaim upgrades from the known. The Revelation gathers intel *and* plants a brick. Every power has a perfect moment — finding it is the skill.

------

## Fatigue (Scoring Across Rounds)

Summit Scramble isn't a single race — it's a **Championship**. Play multiple rounds. Track Fatigue (Zzz's) after each round based on finishing position:

| Place | Status       | Fatigue (Zzz's)          |
| ----- | ------------ | ------------------------ |
| 1st   | Champion     | 0                        |
| 2nd   | Runner-Up    | 2                        |
| 3rd   | Podium       | 4                        |
| 4th   | Field Finish | 6                        |
| Last  | Dreaming     | 8 + 1 per card remaining |

The **Championship** ends when any player's total Fatigue reaches **30 Zzz's** (standard) or **20 Zzz's** (short format). **Lowest total Fatigue wins the title.** The player who triggered the limit doesn't automatically lose — everyone compares totals.

After each round, the player who finished **last** leads the first trick of the next round. (Small consolation. The view from the bottom is still a view.)

> **🛡️ Mercy Rule (Recommended for your first Championship):** Cap maximum Fatigue per round at **12 Zzz's**. This keeps everyone mathematically alive for the title while preserving the competitive sting of a poor finish. Veterans who want the full punishment can play uncapped — but earn it first.

------

## The Stored Surge (Advanced Tactic)

*Skip this section for your first game. Come back when you're hungry for more.*

Veteran climbers know that sometimes the best move isn't using your power *now* — it's saving it for a bigger moment later.

When you win a trick with a **Power Rank card** (6–10), you may **Store** that card instead of triggering its Faction Ability:

1. Place it **face-up** in front of you. This is your **Stored Surge**. (Maximum 1 at a time.)
2. On a **future trick** that you win with any Power Rank (6–10), you may **release your Stored Surge** to trigger the faction ability of your current winning card **twice**. A Double Surge.

The stored card is gone — it doesn't return to your hand. You spent it as fuel for a future explosion.

**Why this matters:** A Double Streamline discards 2 cards in one shot. A Double Rotation forces *two* rounds of card-passing chaos. A Double Revelation lets you scout and swap with two different players. The Stored Surge is the difference between reaching The Summit and falling one card short.

> **The cost is tempo, not cards.** The stored card was already played — your hand isn't affected. What you're giving up is the power *right now*. And everyone at the table can see your stored card, which means they know what's coming. A visible Double Streamline threat changes how opponents play around you. That's the trade: surprise now vs. power later.

## Team Relays (Partnership Mode)

*4 or 6 Players*

For when you want to yell strategy across the table and blame someone else for your mistakes.

**Setup:** Partners sit **across from each other** (not adjacent). Mixed faction teams allowed. Deal cards normally.

**The Huddle:** Partners may **openly discuss strategy** and show each other their hands at any time. This is a relay, not a spy mission. Talk loudly. Plan brazenly. Everyone can hear you anyway.

**Scoring:** **Combined Fatigue** — your team's total is the sum of both partners' scores. Lowest combined total wins.

> **Casual Relay (Optional):** Use **Better Half Scoring** — your team's Fatigue equals the *lower* of the two partners' scores + 2. The stronger runner carries the team spirit when one partner draws a hand full of mid-range garbage.

**The Assist:** You may deliberately **pass** on a trick your partner is currently winning. Let them have it. Preserve their momentum. Teamwork.

### ⚠️ Friendly Fire

Faction Abilities are **indiscriminate**. If The Rotation passes your partner a nightmare card, that's legal. If The Revelation targets your teammate, that's legal. Championship rivalry respects no alliances until The Summit is reached.

This is a feature, not a bug. Friendly fire creates table talk, prevents solved partnerships, and occasionally generates the best arguments your gaming group has ever had.

### 🏆 The Summit Sweep

If one team captures **1st AND 2nd place** in the same round, they execute a **Summit Sweep** — total domination:

- Sweeping team: **0 Fatigue** (perfect execution)
- All other teams: **20 Fatigue** (outclassed by the surge)

Sweeps are rare. Sweeps are devastating. Sweeps are *extremely* satisfying if you're on the right side.

## Solo Challenge: The Night Shift

*1–2 Players*

The Stadium is dark. The crowd is gone. Only the **Night Owls** remain — automated referee drones that climb with mechanical precision. Your job: summit before they exhaust their decks.

**Setup:** You vs. Two Night Owls (Deck A and Deck B). Deal yourself **12 cards**. Split the remaining cards evenly between the two Owl decks, face-down.

### Owl Protocol

Night Owls don't think. They just flip cards from their decks. But they're relentless.

**Turn Order:** You → Owl A → You → Owl B. (Yes, you go twice per cycle. You need the advantage.)

**When an Owl Leads:** The active Owl flips its top card. That's the trick. Beat it or pass.

**When an Owl Follows:** The active Owl flips its top card. If the flipped rank **beats the current high card**, the Owl takes the lead. Otherwise, the Owl rests (its card goes to Base Camp).

**The Strategic Conserve:** When you have **3 or fewer cards**, you may **pass** on a trick you could otherwise beat. This forces the two Owls to race *each other*, burning through their decks while you catch your breath. This tactic is not optional for victory — it's mathematically essential. Learn it. Love it.

**The Trip-Up:** Your Rank 0 cards interrupt an Owl's Rank 10 exactly like in multiplayer. A single 0 beats a single 10 and seizes initiative. Drawing one in your opening hand significantly increases your win probability.

**Trail Exhaustion:** When the Trail empties or can't fulfill a draw, immediately shuffle Base Camp to form a new Trail and continue. Play continues.

### Solo Faction Variants

Since Owls have no hands, **Red** and **Blue** abilities work differently:

| Faction             | Solo Ability         | Effect                                                       |
| ------------------- | -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 🛡️ Super-Dupes (Red) | **The Substitution** | Force the Owl that just played to **skip its next turn**. The other Owl takes the next turn instead. |
| 🎩 Magicians (Blue)  | **The Forecast**     | Look at the **top card of each Owl deck**. You may swap them. |

All other Faction Abilities work normally.

### ⚡ Iron Climb Mode (Grandmaster)

For climbers who have mastered The Strategic Conserve and want suffering:

- Owls **do not rest** when they lose a trick — they keep flipping until the trick resolves or they win
- **Win rate: <5%.** You were warned

## Quick Reference

**Victory:** Lowest total Fatigue when someone reaches 30 Zzz's (or 20 in short format).

**On Your Turn:** Beat the current formation with a higher one of the same type — or pass.

**Formations:**

| Formation       | What It Is           | Beat With                                             |
| --------------- | -------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Solo Sprint     | 1 card               | Higher rank (ties: faction hierarchy)                 |
| Surge           | 2 or 3 of same rank  | Higher rank, same quantity                            |
| Daisy Chain     | 3+ consecutive ranks | Higher top rank, same length                          |
| Confetti Cannon | 4 of same rank       | Higher rank Cannon; triggers 1 power (choose faction) |
| Trip-Up         | Single Rank 0        | Beats Solo Rank 10 only — interrupt, like Cannon      |

**Faction Hierarchy (Ties):** 🔴 Red > 🟠 Orange > 🟡 Yellow > 🟢 Green > 🔵 Blue > 🟣 Purple

**Trick Flow:**

1. Winner leads any formation
2. Clockwise: beat it or pass
3. Pass = out until next trick (Cannons and Trip-Ups always legal)
4. Last standing wins → discard to Base Camp → power check (6+) → lead again

**Faction Abilities (Win a trick with Rank 6–10):**

| Faction  | Ability        | Effect                                                      |
| -------- | -------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| 🛡️ Red    | The Rotation   | Choose left or right; everyone passes 1 card that direction |
| 🔍 Orange | Scout          | Look at top 2 of Trail; may swap 1 from hand with 1 of them |
| 🔧 Yellow | Streamline     | Discard 1 card from hand                                    |
| 🔮 Green  | Recalibrate    | Draw 1, then discard 2 from hand                            |
| 🎩 Blue   | The Revelation | Target player with fewer cards: see hand, take 1, give 1    |
| ⏰ Purple | Reclaim        | Swap 1 from hand with any card in Base Camp                 |

**Stored Surge (Advanced):** Store a 6-10 win card face-up (max 1). Release on future 6-10 win to trigger that power twice.

**Fatigue:** 1st = 0 | 2nd = 2 | 3rd = 4 | 4th = 6 | Last = 8 + 1 per card remaining

## Designer's Note

Summit Scramble is the middle child of this collection — faster than Contests of Chaos, sharper than Get Stuffed. If Get Stuffed is the warm-up round where everyone's yelling and someone just drew 9 cards, Summit Scramble is the focused sprint where every pass is a calculated risk and every Confetti Cannon is a table-shaking event.

Mechanically, this is a climbing game in the Big Two / President / Tichu family. That pedigree is proven — "play bigger or pass" is one of the most elegant structures in card gaming. The H&M deck's six factions and 0–10 spread maps perfectly onto it: enough ranks for meaningful sequences, enough suits for faction hierarchy tie-breaking, and just enough cards per hand to create genuine tension about when to fight and when to fold.

**On Powers:** The 6–10 threshold matches Get Stuffed deliberately. Same deck, same cutoff, different effects. A player who's learned that "high cards do something extra" in Get Stuffed already has the mental model for Summit Scramble. That's the collection doing its job — each game teaches you something that makes the next game easier to learn.

Every power was balanced against one question: *does this help you win the race?* Two powers accelerate (Streamline and Recalibrate shrink your hand), four powers upgrade quality without changing hand size (Scout, Reclaim, Revelation, and Rotation). No power is a penalty. Every trigger creates a genuine decision about whether winning *this*trick is worth the card you'd spend to do it.

**On the Stored Surge:** This is dessert. Skip it for your first three games. When players start saying "I wish I could have used that power *later*" — that's when you introduce it. The Stored Surge rewards planning and creates a visible telegraph — opponents can see your stored card and play around it. It's the expert layer that separates "I know how to play" from "I know how to *win*."

**On the Trip-Up:** A single 0 beating a single 10 is the best moment in the game. Making it an interrupt — playable anytime, even after passing — turns every solo 10 into a table-wide held breath. The restriction (Solo Sprints only, can't interrupt going out) keeps it surgical. Without the restriction, players hoard 0s as universal trump cards and the game degenerates. With it, the 0 is a sniper rifle: devastating when used correctly, dead weight otherwise.

Where Get Stuffed creates chaos and Contests of Chaos rewards calculation, Summit Scramble lives in the tension between aggression and conservation. Every card played is one step closer to The Summit — but also one fewer card in your hand for the fights ahead. The best players know when to burn their hand and when to sit back and let everyone else exhaust each other.

Play hard. Shake hands after.
