# Kahu Fun Audit — All Player Counts

*500 AI-vs-AI simulations per player count. Expert-level heuristic AI, rules as written. March 2026.*

---

## Overall Grades

| Players | Overall | Decision Density | Comeback | Dead Turns | Blowout | Tension | Power Fantasy | Interaction |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **2** | **A** | A | A | A | A | B | A | A |
| **3** | **A** | A | A | A | A | B | A | A |
| **4** | **B** | A | A | A | C | C | A | A |

Kahu plays best at 2 and 3 players, earning straight A's with a single B in tension curve. At 4 players the game still works — five of seven dimensions hold at A — but blowout rate and tension curve both drop to C, pulling the overall to a B. The 4-player experience is noticeably less tight than the 2–3 player game.

## Dimension 1: Decision Density

*How often do players face meaningful choices between distinct action types?*

| Players | Turns with 2+ Choices | Avg Choices/Turn | Grade |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | 87% | 2.4 | A |
| 3 | 87% | 2.4 | A |
| 4 | 87% | 2.3 | A |

Decision density is rock-solid across all counts. On nearly 9 out of 10 turns, players choose between at least two fundamentally different action categories (buy market card vs. buy Pua vs. complete Offering vs. buy Surf vs. claim Tiki). The slight dip at 4 players (2.3 vs. 2.4) reflects slightly more competition for market cards and Pua supply, but it's negligible.

This is Kahu's most consistent strength. The market row, Pua market, Offering display, Surf supply, and Tiki display provide five parallel spending channels, and influence is flexible enough that 2–3 channels are usually live on any given turn.

## Dimension 2: Comeback Potential

*Can trailing players recover? Does the lead change hands?*

| Players | Early Leader Loses | Lead Changes/Game | Last-to-Top-2 Rate | Grade |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | 45% | 5.0 | 100% | A |
| 3 | 55% | 6.6 | 59% | A |
| 4 | 60% | 7.2 | 41% | A |

Comeback potential actually *improves* with more players. The early leader loses 60% of the time at 4 players versus 45% at 2 players. More players means more competition for first-completion VP tokens, more Pua price disruption, and more variance in Offering bonus payouts — all of which break early leads apart.

The 2-player "last-to-top-2" rate is 100% by definition (there are only 2 players, so last place always finishes in the top 2). At 3 players, the halftime trailing player still finishes in the top 2 nearly 60% of the time. At 4 players it drops to 41%, which is still strong — last place at halftime has a better-than-expected shot at finishing near the top.

Lead changes average 5–7 per game, meaning the perceived winner shifts roughly every other round. No player should feel locked out at any point.

## Dimension 3: Dead Turn Rate

*How often do players have nothing productive to do?*

| Players | Avg Dead Rate | Zero-Dead Games | Worst Single Game | Grade |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | 0.0% | 99% | 4% | A |
| 3 | 0.0% | 99% | 3% | A |
| 4 | 0.1% | 97% | 4% | A |

Virtually no dead turns at any player count. Across 1,500 games and roughly 57,000 total turns, the dead turn rate is essentially zero. Even the single worst game in each sample maxed out at 3–4% dead turns (about 1 dead turn in a full game).

This is a direct consequence of the market row: even when a player can't buy Pua or complete an Offering, there's always a market card or Surf card worth purchasing. The guaranteed-available Surf supply at a flat cost of 2 acts as a floor on useful spending.

## Dimension 4: Blowout Rate

*How often are games lopsided? How close are typical finishes?*

| Players | 2× Blowout Rate | Avg Winner Margin | Close Games (≤3 VP) | Grade |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | 0% | 6.4 VP | 34% | A |
| 3 | 4% | 4.5 VP | 50% | A |
| 4 | 11% | 3.6 VP | 56% | C |

This is where the player-count story diverges. At 2 players, blowouts literally never happen — the 2× ratio is 0% — and the margin is a healthy 6.4 VP. At 3 players, margins tighten to 4.5 VP with half of all games within 3 VP.

At 4 players, something shifts. The margin gets even tighter on average (3.6 VP), and more games are close (56% within 3 VP) — but the 2× blowout rate jumps to 11%. This seems contradictory: how can games be closer *and* more blowout-prone? The answer is that 4-player scoring compresses the middle while stretching the extremes. Most games are close three-way races for 2nd and 3rd, but the winner sometimes pulls significantly ahead — particularly Player 0 — creating a bimodal distribution.

The 4-player blowout issue is intertwined with the first-player advantage. Player 0 wins 37% at 4 players (expected: 25%), and their average total score (27.7) is nearly 3 VP higher than Player 3's (24.9). When P0 runs away with the game, it registers as a blowout against P3 and P2 even though P1 may be close behind.

## Dimension 5: Tension Curve

*Does excitement build toward the end? Does the final act feel climactic?*

| Players | Ascending Curve | Final Third VP Share | VP Early → Mid → Late | Grade |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | 43% | 47% | 3.5 → 6.0 → 8.3 | B |
| 3 | 43% | 46% | 4.1 → 8.2 → 10.3 | B |
| 4 | 35% | 44% | 4.7 → 9.8 → 11.6 | C |

VP accelerates toward the final third at all player counts — the right shape. The final third accounts for 44–47% of all VP scored, meaning the endgame delivers the biggest scoring moments. The absolute VP numbers scale naturally with player count.

The issue is consistency. Only 35–43% of individual games show a *strictly* ascending curve. In many games, the mid-game scores a spike (from the first round of Offering completions) that exceeds the late game, or the early game has an unusually productive opening. The tension is there in aggregate, but individual game arcs are inconsistent.

At 4 players, the ascending rate drops to 35% and the final third share to 44%, earning only a C. The 4-player game has more early-game action (four players all completing their first Offerings in rounds 3–5) which front-loads scoring and flattens the overall arc.

## Dimension 6: Power Fantasy

*Do players experience exciting, memorable turns?*

| Players | Games with Wow Turns | Avg Wow/Game | Wow Rate | Grade |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | 100% | 7.8 | ~27% | A |
| 3 | 100% | 9.0 | ~25% | A |
| 4 | 100% | 10.3 | ~22% | A |

Every game at every player count produces wow moments. The per-game count scales with game length — more turns means more opportunities for big turns — but the *rate* stays consistent at roughly 1 in 4 turns.

Wow turns are triggered by: completing an Offering (always a wow moment), buying 3+ cards in one turn (a big engine payoff), or purchasing 3+ Pua in one turn (a burst of resource collection). Offering completions are the most impactful wow moment because they combine resource spending, VP gain, and strategic advancement into a single dramatic action.

This dimension is the most uniformly strong across all player counts.

## Dimension 7: Interaction Rate

*Do players meaningfully affect each other, or are they playing solitaire in parallel?*

| Players | Turns with Interaction | Avg Interactions/Game | Grade |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | 57% | 15.9 | A |
| 3 | 61% | 22.6 | A |
| 4 | 63% | 29.4 | A |

Interaction scales naturally with player count. More players means more Pua market disruption (every purchase shifts prices for everyone), more competition for Offering tokens, and more targets for interactive card effects (Chicken, Nene Goose, mutual Islanders).

The primary interaction channel is the Pua market. Buying any Pua at the 3 or 4 price shifts costs for all players, creating a shared economy where timing matters. Secondary interaction comes from card effects that target opponents (Chicken steals from opponent's deck, Nene Goose copies from opponent's discard).

Even at 2 players — the lowest interaction count — more than half of all turns involve some form of player interaction. This is strong for a deck-building game, a genre that often suffers from "multiplayer solitaire."

## Game Health Metrics

| Metric | 2 Players | 3 Players | 4 Players |
|---|---|---|---|
| Avg game length (turns) | 28.6 | 37.4 | 47.4 |
| Avg game length (rounds) | 14.3 | 12.5 | 11.8 |
| Game length range (turns) | 16–46 | 21–57 | 28–68 |
| End by Offerings | 94.4% | 97.6% | 90.8% |
| End by lava eruption | 5.6% | 2.4% | 9.2% |
| Avg final lava position | 4.9 | 8.0 | 7.3 |
| Avg lava advances | 11.1 | 12.0 | 14.7 |
| Avg influence/turn | 7.0 | 6.7 | 6.5 |

Turn count increases linearly with players (≈12 turns per player), but round count actually *decreases* — 14.3 rounds at 2P vs. 11.8 at 4P. This means the game gets shorter in real decision cycles as more players compete for the same Offerings and Pua supply. The 4-player game feels faster from each player's perspective despite taking longer wall-clock.

Lava eruption is most common at 4 players (9.2%) because four Lava Flow cards cycle through four decks. The lava track starts at 22 for 4P, and with an average of 14.7 advances, it typically ends near position 7 — right around the Pua Price Increase threshold. At 2 players, the lower starting position (16) and fewer advances (11.1) leave lava near position 5, dangerously close to eruption but rarely crossing 0.

## Key Takeaways by Player Count

### 2 Players — Grade A

The tightest experience. Zero blowouts, high decision density, strong comeback potential. The main concern is first-player advantage (59% vs. 41%), which is more pronounced at 2P than at any other count. The lava track sits dangerously close to eruption (avg final position 4.9) but rarely crosses 0, creating good tension. Scores are highest at 2P (~40 VP), meaning deck engines have the most time and space to develop.

### 3 Players — Grade A

The sweet spot. Balanced across all dimensions, with the best tension curve of any count. The first-player advantage (42% vs. 32% vs. 25%) is visible but not extreme. Offerings complete at a satisfying cadence. Half of all games are decided by 3 VP or less. This is the player count Kahu is best balanced for.

### 4 Players — Grade B

Still fun, but cracks appear. Blowout rate rises to 11% (C grade), tension curve flattens (C grade), and the seat advantage is steep (P0: 37% vs. P3: 16%). Lava eruption becomes a real possibility (9.2% of games), and Offering competition intensifies — Card Types is completed over 3 times per game, meaning all four players often complete the same Offering. The game works at 4 but would benefit the most from balance adjustments, particularly to first-player advantage and VP token values.

## Grading Methodology

Each dimension is graded A through F based on thresholds calibrated for competitive card games:

| Grade | Decision Density | Comeback | Dead Turns | Blowout | Tension | Power Fantasy | Interaction |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | ≥85% 2+ choices | ≥40% comeback | <5% dead | <5% 2× blowout | ≥50% ascending | ≥70% wow games | >15% rate |
| B | ≥70% | ≥30% | <10% | <10% | ≥40% ascending | ≥50% | >10% |
| C | ≥50% | ≥20% | <20% | <20% | ≥30% final third | ≥30% | >5% |
| D | ≥30% | ≥10% | <35% | <35% | ≥20% final third | ≥15% | >2% |
| F | <30% | <10% | ≥35% | ≥35% | <20% | <15% | ≤2% |

The overall grade is the average of all seven dimensions mapped back to a letter (A=4, B=3, C=2, D=1, F=0). A threshold of 3.5 earns an A overall; 2.5 earns a B.

All simulations use seeded randomness for full reproducibility. AI players operate at expert skill (1.0) with balanced style and 0.5 aggression.
