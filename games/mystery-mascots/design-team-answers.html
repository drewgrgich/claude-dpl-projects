Mystery Mascots — Design Team Answers

3,000 AI-vs-AI simulated games across 6 targeted batches | 500 games per batch | All questions answered with data

The 30-Second Summary

The game's core identity is strong: hidden allegiances create real tension, the draft is meaningful, and the ascending scoring curve makes late-game rooms genuinely dramatic. The three issues worth addressing are: (1) a ~55-60% bust rate that suppresses big scoring moments, (2) a significant first-player advantage at 3 players, and (3) three faction powers (Blue/Yellow/Purple) that rarely get used because rooms resolve too fast. No dominant strategy exists — bold and sneaky play styles perform nearly equally (30% vs 29%). Skill does matter (experts win 41% vs the 25% baseline), but the gap is modest enough that newcomers stay competitive.

The high accuracy means the AI (and likely human players) mostly only accuse when they're confident — either after seeing someone's allegiance (Green power, exposure) or after observing strong card-play patterns. The +5/−2 risk/reward ratio makes this rational: you only need 29% accuracy to break even.

Design implication: With 3.0 of 4 players getting exposed per game, the hidden-to-exposed transition is a real game arc, not a rare event. This is great for drama. But the 95.6% accuracy suggests accusations are confirmatory rather than deductive — players accuse after they already know. If you want more "gambling" accusations, consider making the penalty milder (−1 instead of −2) or adding a reward for early correct guesses.

Q7 Jordan
Is the Locker Room resolution actually dramatic, or just bookkeeping?
The mechanism works, but the 58% bust rate dampens drama.

The reveal moment is inherently dramatic — flipping three face-down cards is exciting regardless. But 58% of the time, the result is "bust: nobody scores," which feels anticlimactic. When rooms DO score, the average payoff is 10.3 points — significant enough to swing the game.

The structural cause: with 3 cards from 6 factions, the probability of all three being different factions is high (57.6% in a 3-faction room). Three different factions = guaranteed bust.

This is the #1 design lever to pull. Reducing bust rate would simultaneously improve power fantasy (more big scores), tension (more VP in play), and the feeling that your card choices matter. Options: (a) bust rooms score half for each faction, (b) 2-card majority counts (change "most cards" to "most or equal cards with highest sum"), (c) reduce to 5 factions.

Based on ~20 seconds per turn (reasonable for a quick card placement), the raw game time is 7-9 minutes. Add 3-5 minutes for the draft, setup, and scoring, and you land at 10-14 minutes total. Every simulated game finished under 20 minutes.

No dragging risk. The tight turn count (18-35 range with small std dev) means game length is extremely consistent. There's no runaway scenario where a game goes 45 minutes. This is excellent for the "one more game" factor.

A beginner playing against an expert still wins ~20% of the time (vs 25% if everyone were equal). The score gap is only 1.9 points — within the noise of a single lucky room resolution. This means your first game, you have a genuine shot at winning.

These numbers are nearly identical. Drafting your own faction cards is not a winning strategy — because everyone does it, and being too obvious about it gives you away. The game's bluffing mechanism prevents the "obvious best move" from being dominant.

Why the game resists being solved:
  1. Hidden information — you can't optimize what you can't see
  2. Rock-paper-scissors dynamics — bold beats balanced, sneaky beats bold at social deduction, balanced beats sneaky at consistency
  3. Wild flexibility — 0s and 10s can become any faction, creating unpredictable resolutions
  4. Social pressure — the "correct" play often reveals your allegiance, creating a genuine tradeoff between winning and hiding
One caveat: Disruptive play (hate-drafting, interference) is clearly suboptimal at 14.4%. In human play, some disruption is natural, but the game doesn't reward it. If you want disruption to be viable, consider adding a reward for busting rooms that would have scored for opponents.
