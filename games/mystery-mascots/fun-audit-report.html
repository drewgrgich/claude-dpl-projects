Critical Findings & Recommendations

Finding 1: The "Blowout" F is misleading. The 2x blowout ratio fires at 83.6% — but the avg winner margin is only 4.6 points and 76% of games are within 5 points. The ratio looks bad because some players score 0 (their faction never wins a room). In a hidden-allegiance game with 6 factions, this is expected: not every faction will score. The actual score *gaps* are tight, which is healthy. Consider using margin-based blowout measurement instead of ratio-based for this genre.
Issue: 56% bust rate. More than half of rooms tie on majority and score zero. This deflates total scoring and reduces power fantasy moments. Since 3 cards from 6 factions often produce 3 different factions, busts are structurally common.
Suggestion: Consider a partial-scoring bust rule (e.g., each faction scores half its rank sum in a bust) or allow a 2-card majority to score (which also reduces busts at 3 players).
Issue: First-player advantage (33.6% vs 15.6%). Seat 0 wins twice as often as Seat 3. The first player gets to fill rooms first, choosing where to build. This is especially impactful at 3 players (52.8% for Seat 0).
Suggestion: Give later seats a small starting bonus (e.g., Seat 3 starts with 1 extra accusation token or gets to see their draft pack before others).
Issue: Blue/Yellow/Purple powers are rarely used (~0.1% of actions). These powers require having cards already in rooms, but the game moves fast. By the time a player might benefit from moving/rewinding a card, rooms resolve quickly.
Suggestion: Allow Blue/Purple to be used as a "fast action" before placing a card (instead of instead-of), giving them a wider window.
Issue: Red faction scores significantly more on average (8.1 vs 3-5 for others). Red's power (flip face-down card) is strong for information warfare and works even with no cards in rooms, unlike Blue/Yellow/Purple.
Suggestion: This may self-correct in human play where Red becomes a target. Monitor.
What's working great: Decision density (A), comeback potential (A), tension curve (A), and interaction (A) are all excellent. The draft-then-bluff core loop creates genuine tension. The ascending scoring curve means games build to exciting finishes. Hidden allegiances prevent runaway leaders. The game's identity is strong.
