# I'm Using Claude Cowork to Project-Manage My First Kickstarter — Here's What's Actually Working

*A solo game publisher's honest look at what AI can (and can't) do when you're launching a tabletop game with no team, no budget, and no idea what you're doing.*

---

I'm launching a card game on Kickstarter in September. I'm not ready to reveal the details yet — we're calling it **Subject 42** for now — but it's a deck of 66 cards that plays multiple completely different games. It's the kind of project that would normally require a small team or, at minimum, a dangerously overstuffed spreadsheet and a prayer.

I have neither a team nor patience for prayer. So I'm running the business side with an AI project manager.

Before you click away — no, the art isn't AI-generated. My artist draws every card by hand. He's incredible. Once we sign our deal, I can't wait to introduce everyone to the world that he has created. The game design is mine. Playtesting happens at real tables with real people on Wednesday nights. 

What AI is doing for me is the unsexy stuff. The stuff that makes first-time publishers burn out before they ever launch.

## The Problem

When you're a first-time publisher wearing every hat — business, marketing, legal, manufacturing, logistics — things get scattered fast. By February I had five separate planning documents that disagreed with each other. One said launch in August. Another said September. My budget lived in a Google Sheet that referenced numbers from a markdown file that referenced a *different* markdown file. I had action items buried in three places and no clear picture of what I should actually be doing this week.

I needed a project manager. I couldn't afford one. So I tried something different.

## Why This Actually Works (And It's Not Magic)

Wanna know a secret? Launching a tabletop game on Kickstarter: it's not a mystery. The playbook *already exists.* 

Hundreds of creators have done it and then written thousands of words about exactly how they did it — the timelines, the marketing funnels, the manufacturer negotiations, the email list targets, the ad spend, the post-mortems when things went sideways. Jamey Stegmaier literally wrote the book. There are blogs, podcasts, subreddits, Discord servers, and BGG threads dissecting every phase of a campaign launch down to the week.

That's the part I'm sure that people will miss when they hear "I'm using AI for this." I'm not asking AI to invent a Kickstarter strategy from scratch. We're lucky enough to have this playbook thanks to the generosity of others who have shared their journey. What I *don't* have is the time or brainpower to synthesize all of that into a coherent plan, cross-reference it with my specific budget, track the (Drew quickly counts) 47 million things that need to happen before September, and keep the whole machine moving while I'm also designing games, running playtests, and negotiating a contract with my artist.

That's what AI is genuinely good at. Not creativity. Not taste. Not "should I make a game about ghosts or the four millionth deck builder." It's good at taking a mountain of well-documented knowledge and helping one person actually *execute* on it without drowning. The Kickstarter launch process is a known quantity. AI helps me treat it like one.

## What an AI PM Actually Does

I use Claude Cowork, which gives it access to my project files — documents, spreadsheets, task lists. It's a desktop tool that lets Claude work directly with files on my computer rather than just chatting back and forth. Here's what a typical session looks like.

I sit down and say, "Here's what happened since last time." Maybe my artist responded to the contract. Maybe I ran a playtest and got feedback. Maybe I just realized I forgot to send manufacturer quotes. (That last one happens more than I'd like to admit.)

Claude Cowork updates the master plan. Not a separate note I'll have to reconcile later — the actual plan file. In real time. It flags things that are behind schedule, asks what I want to focus on, and keeps me honest about timelines. It's basically a project manager who never gets tired, never forgets what we talked about last Tuesday, and never judges me for having five conflicting planning documents. Okay, it judged me a little for the five planning documents.

In one session we consolidated all five of those documents into a single master plan, reconciled my budget spreadsheet with the plan's numbers, built a backer conversion model with six scenarios, and created a decision framework for what to do if our first marketing test flops. That would've taken me a full weekend to do by hand. It took about two hours.

## The Stuff That Works

**Budget modeling.** I have a spreadsheet with low, mid, and high estimates for every line item — art, legal, prototypes, ads, insurance, a buffer elegantly labeled "In Case Shit." Claude added scenario rows that model different backer conversion rates. What if 2% of my email list backs the project? What if 5%? What if my artist's sizable social media following actually shows up? I can now see exactly how many email subscribers I need to break even under different assumptions. Turns out the answer is "more than I currently have." (That's just math. At least this time the math was useful.)

**Consolidation and cleanup.** Five documents became one. Old files got flagged as redundant. The folder went from "creative chaos" to "actual filing system." There's even a README that reflects what's actually there, which honestly felt like a personal milestone.

**Task management.** Claude maintains a task list in a clean markdown format. I have another AI tool - OpenClaw - that reads that file and syncs it to my Todoist, so I get reminders on my phone. Is this a Rube Goldberg machine? A little. Does it work? Getting there. The point is that tasks surface from conversations automatically instead of living in my head where they go to die.

**Risk framing.** This one surprised me. When I panicked about whether our entire project hinges on a single social media post — we're doing a smoke test with my artist's audience — Claude didn't say "it'll be fine." It built a framework with green, yellow, and red thresholds. Specific numbers. If we hit X, keep going. If we hit Y, adjust the plan. If we hit Z, rethink everything. That kind of structured thinking is exactly what a good PM gives you, and it's exactly what I would've skipped doing on my own because I was too busy worrying about it to actually *think about it*.

## What It Can't Do

It can't tell me if the game is fun. That's what the playtesters are for.

It can't sit across from someone at a playtest and watch their face when they realize they can combo two cards together. It can't negotiate with my artist or convince a manufacturer to lower their unit price. It definitely can't draw the characters on the cards with the level of heart and soul that I want. (And even if it could, that's my artist's job.)

The creative and human parts of this project are irreplaceable. AI handles the scaffolding so I can spend more time on the parts that actually matter — playtesting, design iteration, building a relationship with my artist, figuring out if one of the factions is too powerful. (One of them might be too powerful.)

## The Uncomfortable Part

I'm making a tabletop game. The tabletop community is, broadly speaking, not fans of AI. They rightly worry about AI art flooding the market with generic cash-grab games. About the craft of game design being cheapened. About soulless product replacing handmade passion projects.

And . . . they're not wrong to worry about those things.

But there's a gap between "AI made the art" and "AI helped me organize my budget spreadsheet." I think most people, if they saw exactly what I'm using AI for, would shrug. It's project management. It's task lists and conversion formulas and folder cleanup. It's the reason first-time publishers burn out before they ever launch — and burning out helps nobody.

I'm choosing to be transparent about it because I think the conversation deserves more nuance than "AI good" or "AI bad." The real question is: what are you using it for, and what are you protecting?

I'm protecting the art. I'm protecting the game design. I'm protecting the Wednesday night playtest sessions where someone says "wait, can I do *this*?" and the whole table leans in.

I'm letting AI handle the spreadsheets, the project plan, and keeping my butt in line.
