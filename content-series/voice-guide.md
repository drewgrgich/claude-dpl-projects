# Drew's Writing Voice

## Core Traits

**Conversational authority.** Drew writes like he's sitting across from you explaining something he knows well but doesn't take too seriously. He's confident in his knowledge but loose in his delivery. Never stiff, never academic.

**Parenthetical humor.** Asides in parentheses or em dashes that undercut the sentence just enough to land a joke without derailing the point. "(Don't think about it too hard.)" "(Thanks, some other coach.)" "That's just math. Turns out: nope."

**Fragments as punctuation.** Short declarative fragments break up longer explanations. "Thief steals. Great." "One thing. Not two things. Not zero things. One thing." "Simple. (It is not simple.)" These create rhythm and keep the reader from glazing over.

**Self-aware honesty.** Drew names the hard thing directly and doesn't dress it up. "The games were rarely very fun." "I didn't have good language for it at the time." He treats failures and dead ends as interesting, not embarrassing.

**Metaphors that land and leave.** He doesn't belabor imagery. "Like Michelangelo and the marble" — one line, moves on. "Familiar bones, new muscles, different dragon roars" — three beats, done. "The possibilities don't feel freeing — they feel… loud." Images arrive, do their work, and get out.

**Ellipses for pacing.** Drew uses "..." to create a pause that mimics spoken rhythm. "chip . . . chip . . . chip . . ." "there were other games in here." It gives the reader a beat to sit with an idea.

**Warmth without sentimentality.** Even when writing about something he loves (the game, the creative process, game night with his wife), he stays grounded. No gushing. The emotion comes through in specificity, not adjectives.

**Italics for emphasis and flavor text.** Italics do double duty — emphasis on key concepts and in-world voice/color commentary. "*Brakes are optional. Ethics are flexible. Speed is mandatory.*"

**Direct address to the reader.** "Before you click away —" "If you want to follow along, subscribe." He pulls the reader into the room.

## What to Avoid

- Corporate tone or marketing speak ("leverage," "synergy," "optimize")
- Over-explaining or hedging ("it's worth noting that," "to be clear")
- Excessive structure (too many headers, too many bullet points in prose)
- Inspirational platitudes ("follow your dreams," "believe in the process")
- Apologizing for opinions or choices

## Sentence-Level Patterns

- Longer explanatory sentence → short fragment that reframes or punctuates
- Setup → em dash → punchline or aside
- Rhetorical question answered immediately and bluntly
- Lists of three with escalating absurdity ("Left, right, left! Stop stepping on my tail! Maintain formation!")
