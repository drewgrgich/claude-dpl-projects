# The Project Is on Hold. Here's What Happened.

*When you're building something on the side, "the side" assumes you still have a main thing.*

---

I lost my job this week.

I'm not going to get into the details — I'm signing a separation agreement, and there's a non-disparagement clause, which means I legally can't say anything negative about my former employer even if I wanted to. So I won't. What I *can* say is that it happened, it wasn't my choice, and it changes the math on everything.

Subject 42 is on hold.

## The Timing

Here's the weird part: the timing is almost lucky. Almost.

I was days away from writing a \$600 check to my attorney to convert the term sheet into a formal contract with my artist. Days. If this had happened next week, I'd be \$600 in with a legal obligation and no income to back it up. Instead, I caught the exit ramp right before the toll booth.

My artist was the second call I made after my wife. He gets it. We're good. My attorney was the first email — no work started, no fees owed. Clean pause.

Nothing was spent that can't wait.

## What AI Did That I Didn't Expect

Here's where this gets relevant to the series.

When you get a separation agreement, you get a stack of legal language that essentially says: here's what we'll give you, here's what you give up, sign by this date. Most people either sign it without reading carefully or pay a lawyer $300-500 an hour to review it.

I used Claude Cowork.

I fed it the agreement and asked it to break down every clause — what it means in plain English, what I'm giving up, what's standard, and what's worth discussion. At first, everything seemed straight forward. However, as written, the agreement could have resulted in my waiving some payments that are outstanding. Additionally, Claude was instrumental as a tool for me to rationally and fully discuss my memories of the termination meeting while these memories were still searingly fresh. These contemporaneous notes would be extremely valuable in any necessary discussions and having Claude dispassionately interview me to create these notes was extremely useful. I sent these notes to my former employer and had my questions answered to my satisfaction.

To be clear: Claude didn't *negotiate* my separation. It didn't replace an attorney. What it did was give me a detailed, plain-language map of a document that's dense by nature — so that when I *did* make my decisions, I was making them with open eyes instead of just trusting that "standard" means "fine."

That's the same thing it does for my Kickstarter project. It doesn't make the decisions. It makes sure I understand what I'm deciding.

## What's Actually on Hold

Let me be specific, because "on hold" can mean a lot of things.

**Paused (costs money or requires commitments):**
The attorney contract conversion. Ad spending. The landing page build. The content series on its weekly schedule. Manufacturer negotiations. Everything that requires me to write a check or make a promise I might not be able to keep.

**Still going (costs nothing):**
Playtesting. I can still show up to Wednesday night game nights with a prototype deck and watch people play. Game design refinement — the nightly AI-assisted design sessions that explore new mechanics cost nothing but time. And honestly, thinking. Some of the best design work happens when you're not trying to do it.

**Preserved (picks up where I left off):**
Twenty-three playtests worth of data. Five manufacturer quotes. A nearly-final term sheet. A content series with one article published and a full outline. Social media accounts. A company website. A promo deck. None of that disappears.

## The New Timeline

The original plan was to launch on Kickstarter September 1, 2026. That's gone.

The new target is **March 9, 2027**. It's a Tuesday — which is the best day to launch on Kickstarter, according to basically everyone who's studied it. It's the first full week of March, which is a strong month for crowdfunding campaigns. And it's six days before my wedding anniversary, which means I'll be through the most stressful part of launch week before I need to be a functioning human for dinner.

That gives me roughly five months to find a job and stabilize, then six months to execute the plan. The phases are the same — foundation, art production, content surge, ramp-up, launch — just shifted forward.

## The Thing Nobody Tells You

Every Kickstarter advice blog talks about the timeline. The marketing funnel. The email list targets. What they don't talk about is what happens when life does the thing that life does.

You lose a job. Someone gets sick. The car dies. The thing you were building on evenings and weekends suddenly competes with survival instead of Netflix.

I don't have advice for this part. I don't think there *is* advice for this part. You either pick it back up when you can, or you don't. The project will wait. The files are on my computer. The relationships are intact. The game is still fun — I know because I played it last Tuesday and someone asked if they could keep the prototype.

(I said no. I only have one.)

## What Happens to This Series

I'm not going to pretend I'll keep writing weekly while job hunting. That's a promise I can't make and wouldn't believe if I read it from someone else.

But I'll write when there's something worth writing about. The job search itself is part of the story now — a solo founder hitting pause because the runway disappeared. If you're following along because you're interested in the messy, real version of launching a game, well. It just got messier and more real.

The weekly cadence comes back when the project comes back. Until then, consider this the season finale cliffhanger.

Subject 42 isn't dead. It's sleeping.

And I hear it snores.
