# Hamsters & Monsters — KS1 Term Sheet: Plain-English Guide

**For:** Danny Beck
**From:** Drew (Design Play Labs)

This is a section-by-section walkthrough of the KS1 term sheet in plain English. It's not legal advice — just a reference so you can look up what any part of the deal actually means without having to parse the legalese. If something here doesn't match your understanding of what we agreed to, let's talk about it.

## Section 0 — Parties

**Who's involved.** Design Play Labs (that's me) is the Publisher. You're the Licensor and IP owner. Simple.

## Section 1 — Purpose

**What this document is.** This term sheet covers two things bundled together: (1) I'm commissioning you to create artwork for the game, and (2) you're licensing me the Hamsters & Monsters IP to use in a tabletop card game. It only covers our first Kickstarter campaign (KS1). If we want to do expansions or future products, that's a separate conversation — Section 8 gives us a framework for how that conversation starts.

## Section 2 — Definitions

**The dictionary.** This is where we define the key terms that get used throughout the agreement. The important ones:

- **Licensed Property** — Your Hamsters & Monsters universe: the characters, settings, factions, visual elements. Basically everything you've already created that makes H&M what it is, plus whatever we agree to add later.
- **Product** — The game itself: Hamsters & Monsters: The Chaos Games (base game).
- **Field of Use** — Tabletop card games only. This is a narrow lane. You keep all rights for board games, miniatures, RPGs, merch, apps, video games, film, TV, publishing — everything else. I only get card games.
- **KS1** — Our first Kickstarter campaign, including the pledge manager and late pledges.
- **Net Receipts** — The money I actually collect from selling the game, minus platform fees, payment processing, and refunds. This is the number your royalty is calculated on. It doesn't include shipping or taxes that customers pay — those are pass-through costs I never keep.
- **Franchise Marks** — Your H&M brand: the name, logos, trade dress. These are yours.
- **Product Marks** — The game-specific branding I create (like the game title logo). These are mine.
- **Captured Gross Funding** — The total money that comes in from Kickstarter and the pledge manager before platform fees are deducted. This is what determines your kicker bonus tier (Section 6.2).
- **Effective Date** — The day we both sign the final agreement. A lot of deadlines in this deal are measured from this date.

## Section 3 — Commissioned Works

**The art job.** You're creating the deliverables listed in Exhibit A (faction pieces, card back, box art, marketing pack, social stories) for the fees listed there.

- **3.1 Revisions** — You get two rounds of revisions per deliverable. A "revision" means tweaks to something we've already agreed on directionally — not starting over. If I change my mind on the whole concept, that's new scope and gets billed separately.
- **3.2 Delivery / Acceptance** — You deliver to our shared folder. I have 7 business days to either accept or send back one consolidated list of feedback. If I don't respond in 7 days, it's automatically accepted. This protects you from me sitting on deliverables indefinitely.
- **3.3 Payment** — Payment schedule is in Exhibit A5. Three milestones tied to deliverables.
- **3.4 Hourly Add-Ons** — If we need extra work beyond the fixed-fee scope, it's \$50/hour with a 15-hour monthly cap unless we separately agree to more. I have to approve the hours in writing before you start.
- **3.5 Delivery Delays** — If either of us causes a delay, all the downstream dates slide by the same amount. If we miss the fall 2026 launch window, we automatically bump to spring 2027. If I (the Publisher) cause us to miss two consecutive launch windows, you can terminate the deal with 30 days' notice.

## Section 4 — Independent Counsel Stipend

**Lawyer money.** I'm paying you \$750 at signing to reimburse you for having your own attorney review the deal before you sign. The practical reality is you'll likely need to pay your attorney before we execute, so this is structured as a reimbursement — the \$750 comes to you at signing to cover that cost. It's non-refundable, doesn't come out of any other payment, and doesn't reduce your commissions or royalties. It's just me making sure you have the resources to get independent advice.

## Section 5 — License Grant

**The IP license.** This is the core of the deal beyond the art commission.

- **5.1 Grant** — You're giving me an exclusive, worldwide license to use the H&M IP for a tabletop card game during the term of the deal. "Exclusive" means nobody else (including you) can make an H&M card game while our deal is active.
- **5.1.1 Exclusivity Covenant** — Spells out the exclusivity explicitly: you won't license H&M to anyone else for card games during the term. But you keep all rights for everything that isn't a card game.
- **5.2 Trademark License** — Same idea for the H&M brand name and logos — I can use them on the card game, but not for merch, apps, or anything outside the card game lane.
- **5.2.1 Quality Control** — You get to approve how I use the H&M brand on the box front, rulebook cover, and Kickstarter page header. You have 10 business days to respond; silence means approved. After that first approval, anything that looks materially the same doesn't need re-approval.
- **5.2.2 Registration & Filing** — You handle trademark registration for the H&M brand (your cost). I handle trademark registration for the game-specific marks (my cost). We cooperate if either of us needs help with filings.
- **5.3 Permitted Production Modifications** — I can crop, resize, color-correct, and add typography to your art for production purposes without needing approval each time. I won't alter the substance of your work.
- **5.4 Sublicensing / Assignment** — I can bring in manufacturers, distributors, and service providers as needed (standard for getting a game made). If I sell the company or the game line, I can assign the deal with your written consent (which you can't unreasonably refuse), and the buyer has to assume all obligations. On your side: if you ever sell or transfer the H&M IP during the deal, the new owner has to honor our agreement, and you give me 30 days' notice.
- **5.5 Sell-Off** — If the deal ends (expires or gets terminated), I get 12 months to sell through existing inventory. I still owe you royalties and reports on those sales. This prevents a situation where I have a warehouse full of games and have to destroy them overnight.
- **5.6 Narrative Content Control** — This is about making sure the game's story content stays true to your universe. We're building an IP Style Guide together (Exhibit D) that establishes the rules. For big narrative decisions (new characters, new lore), you have approval rights. For flavor text and marketing copy, you get to review and give input, but I make the final call as long as it's consistent with the Style Guide. For purely mechanical stuff (rules language, component labels), I handle it without needing your sign-off.
- **5.6.1 Style Guide Development** — We have 45 days from signing to build the Style Guide together. If we can't finish in 45 days, we can extend by agreement. If we're still stuck at 90 days, either of us can call for mediation. Until it's done, everything goes through the full approval process.
- **5.6.2 Tiered Approval Structure** — Three tiers. Tier 1 (full approval): big narrative stuff — new characters, factions, canon lore. Tier 2 (consultation): flavor text, marketing copy — you review, I decide, but I take your feedback seriously. Tier 3 (no approval needed): rules text, game mechanics, component labels.
- **5.7 Marketing Assets** — You're providing 12 pieces of existing H&M marketing art that I can use to start promoting the game before the commissioned artwork is done. These are yours (not work-for-hire); I'm just borrowing them. The license to use them expires once all the commissioned art is delivered, at which point I switch over to the new stuff. You get to approve how I use them in each marketing channel before I go public.

## Section 6 — License Economics (KS1)

**The money.** How you get paid beyond the commission fees.

- **6.1 KS1 Royalty** — 5% of Net Receipts. No minimum guarantee for KS1. If I already paid royalties on KS1 units when the Kickstarter money came in, I don't pay again when I sell leftover copies at conventions or online later — that would be double-dipping.
- **6.2 Profit-Share Kicker** — Bonus payments based on how well KS1 does, measured by Captured Gross Funding. \$50K–\$75K = \$1,000 bonus; \$75K–\$150K = \$3,000; \$150K+ = \$6,000. You get the highest tier you qualify for, not all of them stacked. Paid within 90 days after KS1 ends. This is on top of royalties.
- **6.3 Net Receipts** — Restates the definition for clarity. The key point: bundles and add-ons get allocated by their standalone prices, so your royalty isn't diluted by me bundling the game with non-H&M products.
- **6.4 Reporting, Payment, Audit** — I send you quarterly reports (due 45 days after each quarter). Payment follows 30 days later. Minimum payout is \$50 (smaller amounts roll forward). You can audit my books once a year with reasonable notice. If I underpaid by more than 10%, I cover the audit costs.
- **6.5 Assignment / Sublicensing Consideration** — If I sell or sublicense the H&M game rights to someone else for upfront money (as opposed to just selling copies of the game), that money counts as Net Receipts and you get your 5% royalty on it.

## Section 7 — Term and Reversion

**How long the deal lasts, and when you get your rights back.**

- **7.1 KS1 Phase** — The license runs from signing through KS1 fulfillment plus 12 months. "Fulfillment Completion" means either I've shipped 95% of backer rewards, or 18 months have passed since the KS1 campaign closed — whichever comes first.
- **7.2 Reversion Triggers** — Your rights come back (with a sell-off period for me) if: (1) I don't launch KS1 by the target date and don't fix it within 30 days of notice; (2) after fulfillment, the game goes out of print for 12 straight months and I don't fix it within 90 days; (3) I materially fail to report or pay and don't fix it within 45 days; or (4) I go bankrupt. "In Print" means I'm actively selling the game through at least one channel and can ship within 45 days.

## Section 8 — Right of First Negotiation (Future Products)

**What happens if we want to do more.** If either of us wants to make another H&M card game product (expansion, reprint, new game), the interested party sends the other a written notice describing the idea. That starts a 60-day window where we negotiate exclusively with each other. If we can't reach a deal in 60 days, we're both free to talk to other people. This doesn't force either of us to agree — it just means we talk to each other first.

## Section 9 — IG/TikTok Story Campaign

**Social media promotion.** You'll post at least 10 stories during the 10 weeks before KS1 launches, at \$50 per story. The posting schedule gets locked per the milestone in Exhibit A4. You send proof within 24 hours of each post. If a post doesn't go up, you either repost within 7 days or refund that \$50.

## Section 10 — Credits & Portfolio

**Getting credit for your work.** I give you standard artist and IP credit on the game (box, rulebook, campaign page — wherever it's customary). You can show the commissioned artwork in your portfolio after it's been publicly revealed. Before the public reveal, we both keep things confidential.

## Section 11 — Ownership; Logo; Warranties

**Who owns what.**

- **Your stuff** — You own the H&M IP and the Franchise Marks. Full stop. The license lets me use them; it doesn't transfer ownership.
- **My stuff** — I own the game rules, mechanics, and any content I created that doesn't use your IP. I also own the Product Marks (game-specific branding).
- **Game logo** — This is work-for-hire, meaning I own it. But you get consultation rights through the quality control process, and if we terminate before launch, the logo reverts to you along with everything else (Section 12).
- **Warranties** — You warrant that your commissioned work is original and properly licensed — no stolen art, no unlicensed stock assets or fonts. The specific terms of indemnification (what happens if there's a claim) will be worked out in the definitive agreement.

## Section 12 — Publisher Termination for Convenience

**My exit ramp.** I can terminate the deal for any reason with 120 days' notice, but not before fulfillment is complete. If I do, the sell-off period kicks in and I still owe you royalties on those sales.

**Pre-Launch Termination** — If the deal terminates for any reason before KS1 launches, all the art you made (including the game logo) reverts to you. I lose all rights to use it. Commission payments for work you already delivered and I accepted are non-refundable — you earned that money. Payments for work you haven't delivered yet get refunded to me within 30 days.

## Section 13 — Notices and Written Acceptance

**How we communicate officially.** All notices, approvals, and acceptances have to be in writing via email. A verbal "looks good" doesn't count. This protects both of us from "I thought you said yes" disputes.

## Section 14 — Non-Binding; Governing Law

**Legal housekeeping.** This term sheet is non-binding — it's a summary of what we've agreed to in principle, but the real contract is the Definitive Agreement that will follow. Arizona law governs. If we have a dispute, it goes to binding arbitration in Maricopa County, Arizona (faster and cheaper than going to court).
